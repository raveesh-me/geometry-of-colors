…and the perceptually uniform ones that fix them.

00 · The thesis

A color is just a point in space. The hard part is choosing the space.

Every color model is a choice of axes and a choice of scale on those axes. Change the scale and you bend the geometry — distances stretch, straight lines curve. This whole talk follows one thread: how we reshape a cube of light into a cylinder you can reason about, and why the math has to fight human perception the whole way.

Newton→ RGB cube→ HSL cylinder→ CIE XYZ→ CIELAB / LUV→ HSLuv
01 · A short history — Newton

He bent the spectrum into a circle

In 1704 Newton took the linear band of the prism's spectrum and joined its ends — red to violet — into a wheel. That single move turned hue into an angle: a periodic coordinate with no start and no end.

It is the first geometric color model, and every cylinder later in this talk inherits its angular hue.

02 · Axes & scales

Rescale an axis, and the whole graph bends

Equal steps go in along the bottom. A nonlinear scale f(x) = xᵞ maps them to the left axis — and they bunch up. Nothing about the data changed; only the ruler did.

This single move — bending an axis — is how sRGB gamma encodes light, and how CIELAB's cube root squeezes the axis to match the eye. Hold onto it.

02 · Axes & scales

A straight-line map is just a matrix

If your transform keeps the origin fixed and grid lines straight and parallel, it's linear — and every linear map is a matrix. The red and blue arrows are where the axes land.

[ 1.00 0.40 ][ 0.15 1.00 ]

Every RGB→XYZ conversion ahead is one 3×3 matrix. The curved conversions are just the bent axes from the last slide.

03 · The RGB cube

Three channels, one cube

Put R, G and B on three perpendicular axes and every color is a point in a unit cube. Eight corners: black, white, the three primaries and three secondaries. The grey diagonal runs corner to corner.

But these axes describe a monitor's phosphors, not human vision — equal numeric steps are not equal perceived steps. That gap is the whole rest of the talk.

03 · The RGB cube

A cube is a stack of slices

Hold Blue fixed and you're looking at one square face — Red across, Green up. Sweep Blue and you sweep through the whole solid. Indexing a color is just reading three coordinates.

04 · Cube → cylinder

Fitting the cube into a cylinder

Stand the cube on its grey diagonal so black is at the bottom and white at the top. Now height is lightness. Spin the leftover color around that axis and angle becomes hue, radius becomes saturation. Round the hexagon off and you have a cylinder.

04 · Cube → cylinder

HSL — hue, saturation, lightness

This is the model most APIs hand you. It's wonderfully intuitive: pick a hue, dial saturation in and out, slide lightness up and down.

But notice the cylinder is perfectly regular — every hue gets the same radius and the same height. Human vision is not regular. That mismatch is the bug we'll spend the back half fixing.

↻ drag to rotate
04 · Cube → cylinder

The conversion is pure bookkeeping

M = max(R, G, B)   m = min(R, G, B)
C = M − m         (chroma)
L = (M + m) / 2
S = C / (1 − |2L − 1|)
H = 60° × (which channel is max,
      offset by the other two)

No eigenvalues, no integrals — HSL is a geometric repackaging of the same RGB numbers. L is just the midpoint of the brightest and darkest channel.

Which is exactly why it misleads: a midpoint of channel values has almost nothing to do with how bright the color looks. To fix that we have to leave RGB entirely.

05 · CIE XYZ — 1931

A device-independent anchor for every other space

XYZ is built from measurements of the eye's three cone responses. Its primaries are deliberately imaginary, chosen so that every color a human can see lands at positive coordinates — and so that Y is exactly luminance.

Because it's tied to perception, not to any screen, XYZ is the hub: RGB, Lab, Luv and HSLuv all route through it. And — recall slide 02 — getting there from linear RGB is just one matrix.

06 · CIELAB / CIELUV

XYZ measures light, not perception

Each ring marks the same just-noticeable difference in color. If the geometry matched the eye, they'd all be identical circles. Instead — MacAdam's 1942 result — they're ellipses of wildly different size, huge in the green, tiny in the blue.

So Euclidean distance in XYZ lies about perceived difference. CIELAB and CIELUV are the fix: re-bend the axes until equal distance ≈ equal perceived change.

ellipses ×11, illustrative
06 · CIELAB / CIELUV

CIELAB bends the axes with a cube root

with t = X/Xₙ, Y/Yₙ, Z/Zₙ
f(t) = ∛t        if t > ε
f(t) = (κt + 16)/116  else
L* = 116·f(Y/Yₙ) − 16
a* = 500·( f(X/Xₙ) − f(Y/Yₙ) )
b* = 200·( f(Y/Yₙ) − f(Z/Zₙ) )
ε = 0.0089  κ = 903.3

The cube root is the whole trick. Human response to light is roughly a power law, so ∛ compresses the bright end and stretches the dark — exactly the axis-bending from slide 02, now tuned to the eye.

L* is perceived lightness 0–100. a* is green↔red, b* is blue↔yellow. CIELUV does the same job with a different chromaticity; HSLuv is built on its u, v.

06 · CIELAB / CIELUV

The CIELAB solid is lumpy on purpose

Here is every sRGB color placed by its Lab coordinates: L* up the middle, a* and b* spread out the sides. Unlike the tidy HSL cylinder, this shape is irregular — that irregularity is the truth about how colors actually sit in perception.

The payoff: straight-line distance here is ΔE, a real measure of how different two colors look.

06 · CIELAB / CIELUV

Equal steps, two rulers

Same two endpoints, same number of steps. Interpolated in raw sRGB (top) the midtones clump and skip. Interpolated in CIELAB (bottom) the steps are perceptually even — no dead zone in the middle.

This is why you build gradients and palettes in a perceptual space.

07 · The trouble with HSL

Same lightness — wildly different brightness

Every swatch in a row has the same lightness value. Below each color is its true perceived brightness as grey. In HSL the grey strip lurches — yellow blazes, blue sinks. In HSLuv it stays flat.

HSL's L is the midpoint of channel numbers; it never asked the eye. That's why fixed-lightness palettes look uneven and accessible-contrast math breaks.

08 · HSLuv — the payoff

Pour the perceptual solid into a cylinder

Start in CIELUV's perceptually-uniform LCh. At a fixed lightness, the set of displayable colors is this lopsided blob — its reach varies by hue. The outer ring is the maximum chroma the screen can show for each hue.

HSLuv's move: divide chroma by that per-hue maximum. Saturation becomes “how far out of what's possible here,” 0–100 — and the lopsided blob becomes a clean cylinder, with perception preserved.

08 · HSLuv — the payoff

HSL vs HSLuv, side by side

Hue runs across, lightness down, saturation fixed. Read any horizontal row: in HSL the brightness heaves up and down as hue changes; in HSLuv the row holds a steady, even lightness all the way across.

That even row is what makes HSLuv safe for theming and contrast.

08 · HSLuv — the payoff

Where saturation comes from

given perceptual L, H from CIELUV:
C_max = maxChromaForLH(L, H)
S = 100 · C / C_max
L = L*     H = H*

The only hard part is C_max. The RGB gamut, seen from LCh, is bounded by six straight lines (one per cube face). For a given lightness and hue you fire a ray out at that angle and take the nearest line it hits — that distance is the maximum chroma.

Normalise by it and saturation is always a clean 0–100, while L and H stay perceptual. That's the entire idea — and it's a few dozen lines of geometry.

09 · HSLuv in Flutter

Ship it: the hsluv package

pub.dev/packages/hsluv · v1.1.3 · a direct port of the reference implementation

pubspec.yaml
dependencies:
  hsluv: ^1.1.3
color.dart
import 'package:hsluv/hsluv.dart';

// High level — like Flutter's HSLColor
final c = HSLuvColor.fromHSL(14, 72, 62);
final Color flutter = c.toColor();

// Low level — List<double> in, hex out
final hex = Hsluv.hsluvToHex([14, 72, 62]);
final hsl = Hsluv.hexToHsluv('#C96A3F');

Two surfaces: HSLuvColor mirrors Flutter's HSLColor for drop-in use, or call the static Hsluv.* converters directly on List<double>.

One gotcha worth a slide of its own: Flutter's HSLColor puts S and L in 0…1, HSLuv uses 0…100. Don't mix them up.

Use it to generate even tonal ramps, accessible contrast pairs, and theme palettes that don't lurch in brightness as the hue rotates.
